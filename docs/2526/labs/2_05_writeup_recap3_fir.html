Study Overview

Research Question

Do differences in test scores between study conditions differ by the note-taking medium used? If so, how do they differ?

Note Taking: Data Codebook

Description

The data used for this write-up exercise are simulated, drawing on a research paper that explored the association between student note taking, and performance when answering different types of questions. The simulated data are loosely based on the findings of this work, and acted to expand upon the methods and results reported in the paper:

Mueller, P. A., & Oppenheimer, D. M. (2014). The pen is mightier than the keyboard: Advantages of longhand over laptop note taking. Psychological Science, 25(6), 1159–1168. https://doi.org/10.1177/0956797614524581

In the current study, participants were invited to take part in a study investigating the medium of note taking and study time on test scores. The sample comprised of 160 students who took notes on a lecture via one of two mediums - either on a laptop or longhand (i.e., using pen and paper). After watching the lecture and taking notes, they were then randomly allocated to one of four study time conditions, either engaging in no, minimal, moderate, or extensive study of the notes taken on their assigned medium. After engaging in study for their allocated time, participants took a test on the lecture content. The test involved a series of questions, where participants could score a maximum of 100 points.

Data Dictionary

The data in laptop_vs_longhand.csv contain three attributes collected from a simulated sample of \(n=160\) hypothetical individuals, and includes:

Variable Description
test_score Test Score (0-100)
medium Medium of note-taking (levels = Longhand, Laptop)
study Study time (levels = No, Minimal, Moderate, Extensive)

The 3-Act Structure

We need to present our report in three clear sections - think of your sections like the 3 key parts of a play or story - we need to (1) provide some background and scene setting for the reader, (2) present our results in the context of the research question, and (3) present a resolution to our story - relate our findings back to the question we were asked and provide our answer.

Solution : Example Write-Up of Analysis Strategy Section

The notes dataset contained information on 160 participants who took part in a study concerning the role(s) of note taking and study time on test scores. Participants took notes on a lecture via one of two mediums: either on a laptop \((n = 80)\) or long-hand using pen and paper \((n = 80)\). They were then randomly allocated to one of four study time conditions, either engaging in no \((n = 40)\), minimal \((n = 40)\), moderate \((n = 40)\), or extensive \((n = 40)\) study of the notes taken on their assigned medium. Participants then answered a series of questions based on the lecture content. The maximum score was 100, where higher scores reflected better test performance.

Our aim in this report was to address the following research question: Do differences in test scores between study conditions differ by the note-taking medium used? If so, how do they differ?

We checked the data and found that all participant data was complete and that test scores were within a reasonable range (i.e., 0–100). We coded the categorical variables using dummy coding, with “No” as the reference level for study condition and “Longhand” as the reference level for medium.

To investigate whether study condition (No, Minimal, Moderate, Extensive) and note-taking medium (Longhand, Laptop) interacted to influence test scores, we fit the following model, where \(S\) stands for “study” and \(M\), for “medium”:

\[ \begin{align} \text{Test Score} &= \beta_0 \\ &+ \beta_1 \cdot \text{S}_\text{Minimal} \\ &+ \beta_2 \cdot \text{S}_\text{Moderate} \\ &+ \beta_3 \cdot \text{S}_\text{Extensive} \\ &+ \beta_4 \cdot \text{M}_\text{Laptop} \\ &+ \beta_5 \cdot (\text{S}_\text{Minimal} \cdot \text{M}_\text{Laptop}) \\ &+ \beta_6 \cdot (\text{S}_\text{Moderate} \cdot \text{M}_\text{Laptop}) \\ &+ \beta_7 \cdot (\text{S}_\text{Extensive} \cdot \text{M}_\text{Laptop}) \\ &+ \epsilon \end{align} \]

We tested whether there was a significant interaction between study condition and note-taking medium:

\[ H_0: \text{All}~~ \beta_j = 0 ~\text{(for j = 5, 6, 7)} \]

\[ H_1: \text{At least one}~ \beta_j \neq \text{(for j = 5, 6, 7)} \]

We considered effects statistically significant at \(\alpha = .05\). As we were using between-subjects datasets, we assumed independence of our error terms. We also assumed linearity, as all predictor variables were categorical. We assessed the assumption about normality of errors using a Q-Q plot of residuals (points should follow along the diagonal line) and the assumption about equal variance of errors using a residual-vs-fitted-values plot (residuals should be evenly spread across the range of fitted values, where the spread should be constant across the range of fitted values). We will discuss the results of all these analyses in the next section.

In the No and Minimal study conditions, test scores between participants using a laptop or writing longhand are similar, though longhand note-takers show a slight test score advantage over laptop users. However, in the Moderate and Extensive study conditions, participants in the longhand note-taking condition scored dramatically higher than those using laptops. Because of this apparent difference of differences, there may be an interaction between study condition and note-taking medium (see Figure 1).

Test scores were analysed with a 4 (study: no vs minimal vs moderate vs extensive) \(\times\) 2 (medium: longhand vs laptop) categorical interaction model.

The model met assumptions of linearity of association and independence of errors (see Appendix A, top left panel of Figure 3; residuals were randomly scattered with a mean of zero and there was no clear dependence), equal variance of errors (see Appendix A, bottom left panel of Figure 3; there was a constant spread of residuals), and normality of errors (see Appendix A, top right panel of Figure 3; the QQplot showed very little deviation from the diagonal line). The predictors were not correlated with one another, with GVIF scores of 1.

Discussion - What To Include

The discussion should be relatively brief, and should not include any statistical analysis - instead think of the discussion as a conclusion, providing an answer to the research question(s).

Solution : Example Write-Up of Discussion Section

Overall, we were able to reject the null hypothesis that there is no influence from note-taking medium on the effect of different amounts of study on test scores.

Specifically, we found that people who took longhand notes performed better than people who took notes on a laptop, and the advantage of taking longhand notes increased as those note-takers spent more time studying.

Our results suggest that using a laptop to take notes may set learners up for less success, and that engaging in more hours of study is not enough to mitigate this effect.
