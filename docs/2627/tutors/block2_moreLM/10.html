The data in DapR2_S1B2_PracticalPart1.csv contain six attributes collected from a simulated sample of \(n=397\) hypothetical individuals, and includes:

Variable Description
pid Participant ID number
Attendance Total attendance (in days)
Conscientiousness Conscientiousness (Levels: Low, Moderate, High)
Time Time of Class (Levels: 9AM, 10AM, 11AM, 12PM, 1PM, 2PM, 3PM, 4PM)
OnlineAccess Frequency of access to online course materials (Levels: Rarely, Sometimes, Often)
Year Year of Study in University (Y1, Y2, Y3, Y4, MsC, PhD)
NoteMore detail about this dataset

The data used for this write-up exercise are simulated, drawing on a meta-analysis that explored the association between student characteristics and grades. The simulated data are loosely based on the findings of this work, and acted to expand upon the methods and results reported in the paper:

Credé, M., Roch, S. G., & Kieszczynka, U. M. (2010). Class attendance in college: A meta-analytic review of the relationship of class attendance with grades and student characteristics. Review of Educational Research, 80(2), 272-295. https://doi.org/10.3102/0034654310362998

The current study was split into two parts. In the first, researchers were interested in further exploring possible predictors of attendance in university courses. They collected information from 397 students across all years of study (i.e., UG (Y1 - Y4), MSc, and PhD), and recorded their class attendance across the academic year, their level of Conscientiousness (categorized as Low, Moderate, or High), the frequency of which they accessed online course materials (categorized as Rarely, Sometimes, or Often), and the timing of class (categorized as 9AM, 10AM, 11AM, 12PM 1PM, 2PM, 3PM, 4PM). In the second, researchers collected data from 200 students, and recorded their class attendance across the academic year and their final course grade.

RQ: Do people with greater conscientiousness and more advanced years of study attend their university courses more?

Question 4

Introduce your statistical analysis (so the reader can interpret the numbers you will report).

    • Tip: If you’ll report your results using the full regression table for your coefficients, then also include the full regression table for the sensitivity analysis in another Appendix section.

We conducted all statistical analyses using R (R Core Team, 2025), and we will consider effects significant at \(\alpha = .05\).

To address our research question, we fit a linear model predicting attendance (in days) as a function of conscientiousness level and year of study. Conscientiousness was treatment-coded, with “Low” as the reference level (resulting in two dummy variables), and year of study was treatment-coded as well, with “Y1” as the reference level (resulting in five dummy variables). The mathematical model formulation is as follows.

\[ \begin{align} \text{Attendance } =& \beta_0 + (\beta_1 \cdot \text{Consc}_\text{Moderate}) + (\beta_2 \cdot \text{Consc}_\text{High}) ~ + \\ & (\beta_3 \cdot \text{Year}_\text{Y2}) + (\beta_4 \cdot \text{Year}_\text{Y3}) + (\beta_5 \cdot \text{Year}_\text{Y4}) + \\ & (\beta_6 \cdot \text{Year}_\text{MSc}) + (\beta_7 \cdot \text{Year}_\text{PhD}) + \epsilon \\ \end{align} \] We are testing the null hypothesis that all slope coefficients are equal to zero, that is, that there is no association between any predictor and attendance. Formally:

\[ \begin{align} H_0 &: \text{ All } \beta_j = 0 \text{ for } j = 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7\\ H_1 &: \text{ Any } \beta_j \neq 0\\ \end{align} \]

After fitting this model, we checked its assumptions. First, we can assume that associations are linear, because all predictors are categorical. Second, we can also assume that all data points are independent, because each observation comes from a different student. Third, errors appear sufficiently normally-distributed (see Figure B.1 in Appendix B), and fourth, errors also have sufficiently equal variance (see Figure B.2 in Appendix B).

We also checked for multicollinearity between the two predictors. Both GVIF values are close to 1, indicating little variance inflation, so multicollinearity is not a concern for this analysis.

Finally, we checked for influential data points and found three which had a relatively large Cook’s distance compared to the others (about 0.03 or greater). We performed sensitivity analyses, leaving out each one of those data points in turn, and found that none of them impact the pattern of results. Coefficient tables for the sensitivity analysis models are presented in Appendix C.

Focusing first on conscientiousness: if attendance increases as conscientiousness increases, then we would expect the coefficient that compares moderate to low conscientiousness to be positive and the coefficient that compares high to low conscientiousness to be even more positive. That is what we find. Holding year of study constant, somebody with moderate conscientiousness is estimated to attend 10.80 more days, on average, than someone with low conscientiousness (\(b\) = 10.80, 95% CI [8.02, 13.09], \(p\) < .001). Turning now to year of study: if attendance increases as year of study increases, we would expect a similar pattern: positive coefficients which get ever larger for progressively more advanced years of study. We conducted all statistical analyses using R (R Core Team, 2025), and we will consider effects significant at \(\alpha = .05\).

To address our research question, we fit a linear model predicting attendance (in days) as a function of conscientiousness level and year of study. Conscientiousness was treatment-coded, with “Low” as the reference level (resulting in two dummy variables), and year of study was treatment-coded as well, with “Y1” as the reference level (resulting in five dummy variables). The mathematical model formulation is as follows.

\[ \begin{align} \text{Attendance } =& \beta_0 + (\beta_1 \cdot \text{Consc}_\text{Moderate}) + (\beta_2 \cdot \text{Consc}_\text{High}) ~ + \\ & (\beta_3 \cdot \text{Year}_\text{Y2}) + (\beta_4 \cdot \text{Year}_\text{Y3}) + (\beta_5 \cdot \text{Year}_\text{Y4}) + \\ & (\beta_6 \cdot \text{Year}_\text{MSc}) + (\beta_7 \cdot \text{Year}_\text{PhD}) + \epsilon \\ \end{align} \] We are testing the null hypothesis that all slope coefficients are equal to zero, that is, that there is no association between any predictor and attendance. Formally:

\[ \begin{align} H_0 &: \text{ All } \beta_j = 0 \text{ for } j = 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7\\ H_1 &: \text{ Any } \beta_j \neq 0\\ \end{align} \]

After fitting this model, we checked its assumptions. First, we can assume that associations are linear, because all predictors are categorical. Second, we can also assume that all data points are independent, because each observation comes from a different student. Third, errors appear sufficiently normally-distributed (see Figure B.1 in Appendix B), and fourth, errors also have sufficiently equal variance (see Figure B.2 in Appendix B).

We also checked for multicollinearity between the two predictors. Both GVIF values are close to 1, indicating little variance inflation, so multicollinearity is not a concern for this analysis.

Finally, we checked for influential data points and found three which had a relatively large Cook’s distance compared to the others (about 0.03 or greater). We performed sensitivity analyses, leaving out each one of those data points in turn, and found that none of them impact the pattern of results. Coefficient tables for the sensitivity analysis models are presented in Appendix C.

Focusing first on conscientiousness: if attendance increases as conscientiousness increases, then we would expect the coefficient that compares moderate to low conscientiousness to be positive and the coefficient that compares high to low conscientiousness to be even more positive. That is what we find. Holding year of study constant, somebody with moderate conscientiousness is estimated to attend 10.80 more days, on average, than someone with low conscientiousness (\(b\) = 10.80, 95% CI [8.02, 13.09], \(p\) < .001). And someone with high conscientiousness is estimated to attend 18.24 more days (\(b\) = 18.24, 95% CI [15.36, 21.12], \(p\) < .001).

Turning now to year of study: if attendance increases as year of study increases, we would expect a similar pattern: positive coefficients which get ever larger for progressively more advanced years of study. This pattern is borne out in general, though with some variability in the undergraduate years, particularly Y3. See Table 1 for a summary of regression coefficients.
